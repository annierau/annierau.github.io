Before I got a blog, I couldn’t believe people thought they were interesting enough to put their thoughts online. And then I made this blog, and now I can’t believe people walk around the world without publishing their thoughts, because I just would LOVE to know what some people are thinking. I sometimes walk past massive university buildings and wish I could open them up and see Richard Scarry-style cross sections of what everyone is doing and thinking in each classroom and office. How fun would that be? Within a half-mile from my room people manipulate lab rats, write symphonies, preserve mastodon bones, perform open heart surgery, and win NCAA gynmastics meets. And I probably pass them on the sidewalk every day! If you have any interest in my thoughts and activities and gratuitious exclamation points, stick around!

I'm not extrordinary, and I have no particular niche or qualification for a blog, but I decided that my marginally interesting thoughts are worth putting out into the world-- why not? I think I’m writing for a sort of Type-II fun, the kind of thing that is sort of uncomfortable in the moment but enjoyable in retrospect (like running!). I love to *have run* and to *have written*. It’s also worth noting that I'm not thinking about my audience much at all. If I were using a host like WordPress or BlogSpot or Wix, I’d be able to see how many views these have, but I’m not, and though I could maybeee figure it out eventually with enough effort, I don’t think I’m going to. Writing to a very small, unknown audience is a practice in internal validation. This blog, to me, frees creation from performance. And I think it's good for me. Send me an email! Say hi! I would love it! Here we goooo!

The deepest depths of my own memory are in a 21st-century world whirring with VCRs and the internet and carbon-spewing power plants. It’s fascinating and frustrating to fathom a Michigan twelve thousand years ago when the Laurentide ice sheet was making a gradual retreat from the land on which I walk to class, gauging out the five Great Lakes, and carving the hills I struggle to climb on morning runs in the Arb. But on a geological timescale, it’s only been a blink of an eye since the colossal, mile-thick Laurentide ice sheet withdrew from Canada and the Northern United States, depositing Long Island on its way out (Cohen et. al, 2014). Its melting may have caused the worldwide “Noah’s Ark” flood central to the Judeo-Christian worldview, and Genesis 9 continues into a call to dominate over the rest of the biome: “The fear and dread of you will fall on all the beasts of the earth, and on all the birds in the sky… they are given into your hands... Just as I gave you the green plants, I now give you everything.” Sacred religious text and dry geological reports converge only to cue the age of the human development somewhere around the time of the Laurentide’s melting. Out with the ice, and in with the human. It was around this time, 12,000 years ago, that the Holocene commenced, a geological epoch that comes from the Greek for “entirely new.” Though some of today’s geologists consider a new, modern epoch to have begun in recent decades, humans have been significantly impacting the environment for thousands of years-- it’s been the age of humans for the entirety of the Holocene.

Today, the glacier’s paltry remains are a Delaware-sized, 20,000-year-old glacier called the Barnes Ice Cap in northeast Canada, toward Greenland (Lacelle et. al, 2018). The ice slowly melts as the millions of square kilometers of lands it once dominated are mined and plowed by the Homo sapien, sculpted into universities and skyscrapers and Burger Kings. There’s a new king of the mountain, and under his reign, the earth looks different than it did during the Pleistocene epoch. It’s an age of a species at the top of the food chain acting invincible, an age of nuclear power and unbridled plastic production and anthropogenic climate change. Though the Holocene epoch only started about 12,000 years ago (ICS, 2020), some geologists look at the past century of human activity’s environmental impact and call for a new epoch altogether. It is— according to some of the diligent geologists who spend endless hours determining geologic eons, eras, periods, epochs and ages— the Anthropocene, a rough translation from the Greek words for “new,” “human,” and “era.”

The name was popularized by a prominent atmospheric chemist named Paul Crutzen to a newly christened twenty-first century whirring with VCRs and the internet and carbon-spewing power plants. And in many climate-minded circles, it stuck, and while it’s not a formally-recognized term, it’s approaching (if not already incorporated into) the vernacular. (In a quick poll of about 20 friends, roommates, and family members, about a quarter could define the Anthropocene). But just because people call this the Anthropocene doesn’t mean it’s the Anthropocene. The word has squiggly red lines in Microsoft Word and Google Docs. The process to becoming a bona fide geological epoch is far more complicated and intensive than one might think: there’s a whole field of geochronology in which decorated academics move back and forth between field stations and board rooms, arguing about the geological time scale and the markers that divide the different eons, eras, periods, epochs and ages.

For an epoch like the Anthropocene to be established, it must withstand the International Council of Stratigraphy’s careful review and analysis, which can take decades. But the prestigious group certainly think the Anthropocene has potential. The ICS established the Anthropocene Working Group in 2009 (9 years after Paul Crutzen’s first documented call for an Anthropocene) to investigate whether the new epoch deserved a place in stratigraphic time (Meyer, 2018). Debates continue. The group focuses on identifying a definitive geologic marker; there are over 100 time divisions in earth’s 4.6 billion year history, and each is matched with a Global Boundary Stratotype Section and Point, abbreviated GSSP, and nicknamed ‘golden spike:’ a clear marker of geological change (ICS, 2020). It’s worth noting that record of ocean temperature or acidity, atmospheric carbon dioxide rise, or glacier melting doesn’t constitute a golden spike: it has to be geological, because physical evidence in the sedimentary record is the basis of geological time divisions.

While we await the next reports from the Anthropocene Working Group, we remain in the Holocene, the last formally recognized epoch Holocene, which started around 11,700 years ago (ICS, 2020): the time when the Laurentide receded from North America. Fantastic megafauna— massive beasts like mammoths, mastodons, giant beavers, and the giant ground sloth— died at the hands of man (Holcombe arrow points indicate human hunting about 11,000 years ago)(UM Museum of Natural History, 2020), or a warming environment, or both. It’s around the time when modern agriculture arose, and that first manipulation of the biome spurred an Agricultural Revolution which led to cities and complex cultures and a boom of technology. Around The Holocene has witnessed all of humanity's recorded history and the rise and fall of all its civilizations. The golden spike marking the start of the Holocene is 1500 meters deep into an ice core in central Greenland, at a spot where a shift in quantities of the hydrogen isotope deuterium reflect the end of a cold spell-- the warming that melted the bulk of the Laurentide glacier. (ICS,2020) And while the Holocene has only been around for about 12,000 years (a mere blip in time compared the 2.5 million span of the Pleistocene, the epoch before it), some geologists like Paul Crutzen look at current earth conditions and see a shift worth noting in official chronostratigraphy. Out with the Holocene, in with the Anthropocene.

Perhaps the biggest contender for Anthropocene start date is 1950, a clean, even year with close proximity to a slew of impactful human activities, including nuclear testing. Humans detonated the very first nuclear bomb in July of 1945 in the New Mexico desert, which scattered radioactive particles from the poles to the equator. This continued worldwide until the Limited Nuclear Test Ban Treaty of 1963, leaving an indelible mark on the surface of the Earth which can be traced back to that 18 year period (Waters et. al., 2015). 1950 also has significance related to anthropogenic climate change: a post-World War II emerging middle class indulged in factories and cars. Industry soared, alone with methane pollution, CO2 emissions, and plastic production. Humans hardly had to toil for food, with automatic cow milkers and John Deere tractors and an ever-more-centralized agricultural-industrial complex. Rock and soil moved around the planet at unprecedented rates (Meyer, 2018). The mid-century industrialization is nicknamed the Great Acceleration, due to the zooming, exponential rise of various human activities ranging from population and GDP to pollutants and temperature. 1950 is a year in living memory. It’s when my grandparents turned 8, and the world may not have been whirring with VCRs and the internet, but it had carbon-spewing power plants and radios and nuclear testing.

Paul Crutzen, who first proposed the Anthropocene in an opinion piece, considered the epoch to start a couple centuries earlier, in the late 1700s, around James Watt’s 1984 steam engine invention. Data retrieved from ice cores show a growth in greenhouse gasses like carbon dioxide and methane (Dawson, 2016), and this date is early enough to include the rise of factories in the western world and the entirety of the Industrial Revolution. The ICS epoch divisions are generally in the millions of years; looking back in time, the past five have been 1.6, 3.7, 18.4, 12.9, and 21.2 million years (ICS, 2020). While there’s value in finding the most perfect date, especially for a group as notoriously careful as the International Council of Stratigraphy, a difference of 200 years is tiny in comparison-- it’s only .0002 million years. Overall, most Anthropocene proponents consider a state date during or after the Industrial Revolution, give or take a few hundred years.

But maybe the environmental impact of the behaviorally modern human is further spread throughout time. The earliest proposed Anthropocene start dates are the beginning of the Agricultural Revolution 12,000-15,000 years ago. Four of the twenty-nine members of the Anthropocene Working Group would like, essentially, for the Anthropocene to supplant the Holocene, using its golden spike and start date (Meyer, 2018). It’s based on the idea that humans have had significant impact on their environment since they first controlled the biome with organized farming, transforming ecosystems, plant species, and (sometimes very large!) animals species. It would be a geological epoch starting when H. sapiens numbered barely 5 million and developed prehistoric agriculture, and the epoch would continue to include the following thousands of years of progressive impacts of humans on the earth’s systems, including all the booms of the Great Acceleration: population, fossil fuel burning, greenhouse gas production, and nuclear activity, and remain in place indefinitely. Whether the epoch would keep the name Holocene or adopt Anthropocene is unclear.

But beyond the weeds of golden spikes and subcommittees is a larger question of the importance of geochronology as a whole. Will a new geological epoch mean anything to anyone but geologists? Will it steer human behavior toward sustainability? The general consensus is no, that it will not change much of anything, because it’s a geological distinction meant for geologists, not meant for a news cycle that runs on sensationalism or for galvanizing a population complacent on climate change. The ongoing debate about the geological epoch may distract from the issue at hand: that humans are burning fossil fuels at an alarming rate.

At worst, the continued argument on whether the Age of Humans has started yet may be misconstrued into the unfounded idea that scientists disagree on whether humans are causing climate change. When the Anthropocene is summed up as “the hotly-debated epoch proposed to include the time that human activity has been the dominant influence on climate and the environment,” the layperson could very easily assume that all the debate is about whether humans are truly the dominant influence on the environment. The presumed discord would fit with the manufactured idea that climate scientists are divided on anthropogenic climate change, the justification for swaths of climate change deniers’ beliefs. We’re past that point. Over 97% of actively publishing climate scientists agree that humans have caused this planet’s warming: a clear consensus (NASA). In reality, the Anthropocene debate is whether the best golden spike lies in a Chinese lake, cave in northern Italy, corals in the Great Barrier Reef, or another one of the ten options the Anthropocene Working Group considers (Meyer, 2018).

An unusual-- and problematic-- aspect of the Anthropocene debate is that it started with Crutzen’s idea that the Homo sapien has made a significant enough impact on a 4.6 billion-year-old earth to warrant an epoch, and then geologists hunted for a golden spike in the rock record to mark the potential change. That’s not the usual order: previous changes to the chronostratigraphy have begun with an interesting natural finding and later resulted in a formal change. Deciding the Age of Humans based on historical cultures and developments and then searching for a geological mark that coincides seems problematic, since major human impacts may not have geological basis. Throwing together a makeshift golden spike would shift away from the precedent the International Council of Stratigraphy has set of using strictly geological evidence to divide earth’s history into time units which don’t always correspond to “important” events. The Oxygen Revolution 2.6 million years ago, a rapid spew of oxygen by oceanic photosynthesizers, led to a mass extinction and transformed the atmosphere (Lyons, 2014). But since it didn’t have major effects on the rock record, it didn’t warrant a distinction in geological time.

Sticking with the Holocene’s golden spike in Greenland’s ice core and forgetting about a brand-new, 20th century epoch doesn’t discount the hugely impactful and destructive effects of human activity on the environment, but upholds epoch length consistency and keeps the ICS strictly-geological. It includes human industrialization, but starts at a crucial shift in human evolution to give perspective to recent anthropogenic climate change.

It’s been 300,000 years since the Homo sapien emerged: a species of big-brained clumsy mammals from a lineage of small-brained clumsy mammals, and those early anatomical humans spent the first 100,000 years of their existence confined to a small area in the dry savanna of East Africa making little culture or social change (Mann, 2012). And then something like 50,000 years ago, our distant ancestors spend tens of thousands of years developing language, culture, art, and religion— what’s called behavioral modernity. These abstract thoughts, artistic inclinations, and propensities to search for a higher meaning, perhaps from a deity rumored to have commanded a man and his family to bring animal pairs onto an ark. Behavioral modernity allowed for innovation, planning, and around 12,000 years ago, organized farming (Mann), which happened right after the Laurentide glacier receded most recently, a crucial shift that led the ICS to declare on the Holocene Epoch (ICS, 2020).

The current-day effects of climate change that we see are snowballed effects of a shift toward civilization we saw at the start of the Holocene. Perhaps it’s been the Anthropocene-- the age of humans-- for a very long time, ever since the Laurentide last carved North America. The ongoing debate continues, and a July 2018 ICS report split the Holocene into three ages, with the last, the Meghalayan, ending at 1950 to give room for a potential Anthropocene. The Working Group prepares for a final report in 2021 (Subramanian, 2019). But while the distinguished scientists in the Group consider the various options for golden spikes, spend time in field stations and offices, reading stacks of paper and vials of dirt, the planet warms due to the greenhouse gasses humans spew into the atmosphere to continue the industrialization that began when the last glacier receded, at the start of the Holocene.

Outdoor Voices broke into the saturated athleisure market by selling a lifestyle that “frees fitness from performance,” that “gets the world moving,” and above all, promotes #DoingThings, especially in slogan-clad cobalt-blue hats. Years ago, I came across the emerging company’s effortlessly beautiful grid lays of minimalist heathered workout clothes, employee yoga breaks, and ethnically diverse, unedited models who were proud of their cellulite. And I consciously crafted my identify to fit their sunny, sweaty, Instagram-square-sized mold.

When they advertised their campus ambassador program, I applied immediately in hopes of free clothes, and soon received an email saying that they liked my content and wanted me on the Crew, signed with “high fives, gracie,” which made me feel simultaneously validated and disconcerted as I realized that I was going to allow a brand, albeit one I unashamedly loved, to essentially purchase posts I made on a medium I’d long considered a personal outlet. I succumbed to selling my personality and downloaded the required app, called SocialLadder, which gave me challenges, such as tagging @outdoorvoices in a grid post for a payout of the point-equivalent of $40 in store credit. The app also had a feed of other influencer’s posts, and ranked us by points accumulated. I didn’t rank highly. Even though I identified with the brand’s mission, I remained a bit conflicted about joining the influencer culture of inauthenticity and, from a waste standpoint, encouraging people to spend too much money on clothes they probably don’t need, even if the company was at least moving in the direction of environmental sustainability.

Instagram marketing mimics age-old word-of-mouth advertising, especially in cases of “nano-influencers” like me with fewer than 2000 followers, but injects it with a cash flow and dangles the product a click away from a user’s feed: Instagram’s “Checkout” feature allows for impulse buys within the app, and has proven wildly popular in the months since its May 2019 limited release. Influencer generated content has proven supremely valuable, often outperforming brand-created content, causing vast majority of brands investing in influencer content planned to increase their influencer spending the following year.

Influener territory isn’t quite the Wild West anymore, but regulation is still spotty at best. In the portion of influencers that I, personally, have seen following these guidelines (one is “don’t mix your disclosure into a group of hashtags or links”) is miniscule at best, probably less than one percent (I haven’t found any objective data). Outdoor Voices never mentioned anything about disclosure to me.

The August WSJ essay “The Lonely Burden of Today’s Teenage Girls” examined young females’ role as “unwitting guinea pigs in today’s huge unplanned experiment with social media.” Teen anxiety, depression, and eating disorder rates really take off around 2013, the year I graduated eighth grade, as Instagram became popular. An app I once thought frivolous takes on new weight. Sociologists’ academic analyses of teenage-girl Instagram psyche strike a hauntingly familiar chord for me: I was twelve when I got the app, and my construction of my identify is meticulously documented in squares I posted throughout my entire adolescence. It’s shown me news in real time, facilitated the formation of niche subcultures, but has also caused me-- and thousands of others-- anxiety and body dysmorphia. I’m also not sure how influencers affect Instagram’s effects on adolescent mental health. Overall, I think I’m glad I signed on with Outdoor Voices. In my four months, I got about $500 worth of Outdoor Voices product and gained a lot of understanding and respect for the mission, their fitness outreach programs, and their sustainable efforts. I’m ashamed that it all came to an end because of my laziness. The final few minutes of the show are the highly-anticipated community reveal, which drives the urgency of the rest of the show (“just three hours before the guests arrive and we still can’t find the right blinds”). But they always are ready in time, and the scores of guests rave about Irvine’s changes in camera interviews. The whole show is predictable; it is manufactured; it is everything I wanted it to be.

wtf is tapioca?!-------------------------------------------->


Tapioca has followed me through alllll sorts of dietary chapters of life: It’s been phlegmy pellets in lunchbox pudding, a first ingredient in vegan cheeses like Daiya (whose proud claims to stretch and melt and provide “undeniable gooey-gooey cheesy texture” are tinged with desperate exaggeration, but still repeatedly convince me to spend $6 on 7-oz bags of mozzarella-style shreds), and small-marble-sized pearls that give chewy contrast to boba tea, my faaaavorite Ann Arbor splurge. But what even is it?

I, Annie Rauwerda, girl detective, decided to Google It. Turns out tapioca comes from the storage roots of the cassava plant (also known as yuca root, which I think is familiar from ingredient lists) that looks like a dirty, off-white carrot, a tuber which contains little more than water and starch. And it’s far more popular than I realized. Cassava is the third-largest source of food carbohydrates in the tropics, after rice and maize (http://www.fao.org/ag/agp/agpc/gcds/). 800 million people depend on cassava as their primary food staple. (http://www.fao.org/3/a-i3278e.pdf), which I think is crazy (though I’m not *entirely* sure what it means [what is MY primary food staple?]). Cassava’s long harvesting window and success on poor soils and with low rainfall make it a renowned famine reserve, and during WWII many refugees in Southeast Asia survived on tapioca. And to think I spent my whole life thinking it was just a more controversial version of vanilla pudding.

Another thing I could hardly believe is the breadth of forms and purposes the root takes: it’s made into alcohol in a number of tropical regions; its cellulose can be processed into biodegradable plastic alternatives; it can be made into tapioca flour, a valuable gluten-free substitute; it’s fried into chips in various Asian countries; it acts as a glue to hold pills together.

It’s as perplexingly versatile as soy. It can become anything, but by itself, tapioca means almost nothing to most people. One could draw similarities between the tapioca and me during sophomore year of college, but I don’t want to get into it. Maybe in another post. Anyway, I feel like a better person for looking this up, but I’m not sure why.

Grades suck, mostly


December 2019. 5 minute read.

bombed a final and my grade dropped which is literally fine but I was upset and dramatic.
---------------------------------------------------->

December 2019. 5 minute read.

bombed a final and my grade dropped which is literally fine but I was upset and dramatic.
---------------------------------------------------->


Nobody knows who invented the numerical marks that practically define the college experience. Academic performance metrics cropped up at multiple institutions at the end of the 16th century. They weren’t standardized yet: Yale’s 1785 graduating class first performanced-based distinctions were the Latin words optimi, inferior, and pejor. But almost 250 years later, the 4.0 scale GPA has become as pervasive to US higher education as the red solo cup, and most students first became familiar with the fateful first four letters of the alphabet shortly after they learned them in preschool. The average GPA for students at four-year colleges is 3.15, a little higher than a B average. At the University of Michigan, that average is 3.37, a number that has risen very steadily since the data begins in 1933, when average GPA was a measly 2.51. This growth can be attributed, unconvincingly, to a change in raw intellect and study habits, or, more interestingly, to the growing notion that an expensive education should yield better preparation for the workforce and grad school. Students (and parents) interested in getting their money’s worth, and administrators desperate to reverse declining enrollment trends, may put pressure on professors to round up or grade loosely. When the standard for being considered an “above average” student is putzing but the true average student’s GPA is cruising up, grade inflation helps students get ahead-- in this world of changing baselines, the majority of students have “above average” grades. The change generally is supported by students, but exceptions exist. Ted Kaczynski, the Unabomber, said his "memories of the University of Michigan are NOT pleasant ... the fact that I not only passed my courses (except one physics course) but got quite a few As, shows how wretchedly low the standards were at Michigan."

During my sophomore year of high school, my entire district’s grading scale slid back in the interest of college admissions; the once-elusive 97-100% A became an entirely doable 93%. Another response to this pressure to maximize students’ post-grad opportunities is to forgo the numerical performance summaries altogether; notable examples of grade-eschewing institutions are Brown University and uberpresitigious Saint Ann’s High School in Brooklyn. One headline quipped that trend is to go from “degrading to de-grading.” Notably, schools that make this decision tend to have prestige and alternativeness to spare: a status that isn’t the case everywhere.

When I worked for the Chicago Public Schools, I found myself grading for the first time after spending twelve years as a student. I also had to make sense of the standards used upon me as a school employee. The 2017-2018 CPS School Quality Rating Policy was the battered remains of a 1995 Illinois mandate to give schools stakes, and the system gave grades to the schools themselves: level 1+ being the best, and level 3 indicating probationary status. Tied to high ratings were funding and glory; to low ratings, district and state meddling. My school’s ranking had just fallen from 1 to 2+, and the administrators’ damage control mindset was palpable— so much that, when my partner teacher submitted failing students’ failing grades, the principal asked her to change the Fs to Cs.

I imagine that whoever came up with grades (I imagine them as a singular, contemplative old man in an armchair) envisioned the numbers and letters serving all sorts of purposes: they can be clear metrics of failing students, signaling to schools and families a need for individual intervention. They’re also a track record for higher education institutions and potential employers: proof of responsibility and work ethic or their absence. Most familiar to me— yet still entirely perplexing-- is the aim of the GPA to give students themselves higher stakes to their performance: a system that translates the first letter of the alphabet into a pat on the back and the fourth to a cold shower or slap on the wrist. And that’s ideal-- that a student can recover from a bad grade. That’s why my partner teacher attempted to fail the students who openly mocked the subject, put no effort into their work, and rarely turned in an assignment. There’s a justice to it all. A bad grade should be a call to turn behavior around and to set goals. But in aggregate, GPA get tangled with self-esteem, reputation, and dignity. To some students who lack self-efficacy, or support, or maturity to try again, bad grades can seem out of their own control, can spur a learned helplessness, and give a kid the idea that there is no path to redemption from, say, a 1.5 cumulative GPA.

Which is probably why the 2017-2018 CPS School Quality Rating Policy designers penalized schools for failing too many students (ie, if schools gave too many failing grades, they would get a failing grade). It’s why the school administrators didn’t let me partner teacher give Fs to that handful of underperforming 11-year-olds in 5th grade math class. It’s a restorative approach to grading; unfortunately, the grade inflation seemed to validate the students’ misbehavior and apathy. There’s no easy solution.

Single-faceted school evaluations can backfire too: massive standardized test scandals have arisen around the country. The stakes over standardized testing ratchet higher, legacies of No Child Left Behind’s federal education evaluation. In response to the pressure (standardized test scores are often a factor in teachers’ performance reviews, and can translate to higher salary), teachers are increasingly caught cheating on their students’ tests, changing answers in completed test booklets or give a peeked-at-test-question inspired lesson before the testing. Steven Levitt of Freakonomics fame authored a paper describing Chicago students having strings of identical answers in 2002, pointing to foul play-- literally erasing and pencilling in new answers-- by about 5% of teachers. A failure of incentives.

Now that I am a student again, grades are all-consuming. I took relatively easy classes my first semester and wound up with a 4.0 and found validation and motivation in that clean number— a sense of worth that, in the midst of a demanding course load, didn’t lull me into complacency but inspired me to maintain the beloved badge of honor known only to my parents and most observant LinkedIn connections. My 4.0 was whizzing momentum. And then it dipped to 3.98. If I could go back in time with what I know now, I’d start studying for my Chemistry final weeks in advance. In this sense, the dash next to that A is the cold shower it should be. I learned something.

I loosen the knot tying my GPA to my ego; I employ the rational thinking that’s gotten me this far, and I remind myself that GPA— like most educational attainment metrics— is probably some dead old man’s imperfect, workable, well-intentioned idea.

Amway sucks, mostly


January 2020. 3 minute read.

---------------------------------------------------->

January 2020. 3 minute read.

---------------------------------------------------->


I grew up Grand Rapids, Michigan, home of Amway’s sprawling global headquarters since 1959. The company’s heirs have poured their legacies into West Michigan institutions, building a newer, more beautiful skyline of state-of-the-art hospitals, hotels, arenas, and schools, usually plastered with their familiar last names: DeVos and Van Andel. Descendents with the same last names populate my schools and churches, they’re my friends and colleagues. When Betsy Devos was catapulted into Trump-cabinet-notoriety, many individuals in my West Michigan circles cringed yet held their tongues, knowing that the wealth of her family funds our city.

Amway independent sellers dot the globe selling health and cleaning and beauty products, but since they don’t sell in Grand Rapids, I didn’t understand what the company did until I was probably twenty. It’s the first and largest¹ company of its kind, and particularly notable for its aggressive international expansion, entering 100 countries, mostly developing nations, since the 70s². The company is hinged on the American Dream, or the American Way, the phrase it adopted as their trademarked portmanteau: Amway. They sell products to people, but more importantly, sell an idea to sellers: that anyone can build a business with the right tools. It is this mindset that leads success-hungry individuals to buy-in, to mine their social circles for friends and family members willing to purchase products or join the Amway ranks, and work their way up the Amway pyramid.

According to a 2011 FTC report, federally-funded research through the Consumer Awareness Institute, 99% of sellers accrued an overall loss for all of the MLMs for which relevant data could be obtained. Promoting the practice as an “income” or “business” opportunity is, according to the report, “a misrepresentation in itself.” Amway’s business model has been outlawed in the UK³, India⁴, and China⁵. Corners of the internet are fiercely hostile toward MLMs, such as r/antiMLM on Reddit and the 2018 podcast “The Dream.” Despite lawsuits⁵ and backlash to Amway’s intense lobbying⁶, the corporate behemoth continues unscathed: Amway keeps selling its sellers the “tools for success”, who sell their Facebook friends marked-up nutrient shakes and household soaps and leggings.

The world of MLMs is intertwined with the Information Age: most independent sellers primarily advertise their business on FB, (sometimes with posts peppered with emojis and tinged with desperation). With all the post data available, I’m surprised to find so little research published on MLMs and I’d be curious to confirm the 1% success rate, determine average seller losses, time spent in the scheme, and what successful sellers have in common, especially considering so many scammed sellers often already struggle financially, enough to obtain a side hustle. Because independent seller work is done online, remotely, and often from smartphones, the gig attracts many single moms. I’m excited that the BSI could enable me to use data sets from Facebook or other places to encourage business transparency and allow potential-sellers to make informed decisions.
