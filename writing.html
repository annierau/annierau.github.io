the rise of the grade point average

december, 2019. 4 minute read.

I’m 20 years old, and I got my first college A- today. My 4.0's demise was inevitable, but it still stings, especially because I didn't see it coming (I bombed a final). Now I can’t stop thinking about grades.

Nobody knows who invented the numerical marks that practically define the college experience, but they've been around for centuries. Academic performance metrics cropped up at multiple institutions at the end of the 16th century. They weren’t standardized yet: Yale’s 1785 graduating class's first performanced-based distinctions were the Latin words optimi, inferior, and pejor. But almost 250 years later, the 4.0 scale GPA has become as pervasive to US higher education as the red solo cup, and most students first became familiar with the fateful first four letters of the alphabet shortly after they learned them in preschool. The average GPA for students at four-year colleges is 3.15, a little higher than a B average. At the University of Michigan, that average is 3.37, a number that has risen very steadily since the data begins in 1933, when average GPA was a measly 2.51.

This growth can be attributed, unconvincingly, to a change in raw intellect and study habits, or, more interestingly, to the growing notion that an expensive education should yield better preparation for the workforce and grad school. Students (and parents) interested in getting their money’s worth, and administrators desperate to reverse declining enrollment trends, may put pressure on professors to round up or grade loosely. When the standard for being considered an “above average” student is putzing but the true average student’s GPA is rising steadily, grade inflation helps students get ahead-- in this world of changing baselines, the majority of students have “above average” grades. During my sophomore year of high school, my entire district’s grading scale slid back in the interest of college admissions; the once-elusive 97-100% A became an entirely doable 93%.

more than you wanted to know about restaurant impossible

may, 2019. 4 minute read.

Each episode of Restaurant: Impossible opens with a clip of celebrity chef Robert Irvine shaking his head in disapproval and crossing his arms, pulling his already skin-tight short sleeves even tighter around his yoked-out biceps. “I’m just waiting for the sleeves to rip,” my friend’s dad would say to the neighborhood girls and me as we camped our 10-year-old selves out in front of Food Network on winter weekend mornings. “Chef Robert Irvine has 2 days and $10,000 to do the impossible!” the TV would announce, conjuring suspense as we awaited Irvine’s valiant restaurant rescue.

There’s a lazy satisfaction from procedurals or makeover-type shows, where I can watch other people dutifully solve problems from the comfort of my couch. Each loose end is tied up within forty-two minutes, not including ads. With 16 seasons, the show is nearly inexhaustible for viewers, each episode following the same formula. Restaurant: Impossible’s team would travel, usually to Middle America, to make dingy (and often deeply indebted) restaurants hip and Yelp-worthy by slapping on new wallpaper and making over the menu. The final few minutes of the show are the highly-anticipated community reveal, which drives the urgency of the rest of the show (“just three hours before the guests arrive and we still can’t find the right blinds”). But they always are ready in time, and the scores of guests rave about Irvine’s changes in camera interviews. The whole show is predictable; it is manufactured; it is everything I want it to be.

So during a busride Facebook scroll, when I saw a post calling for volunteers and dinner guests for a GRAND RAPIDS (MY HOMETOWN) episode, I immediately reserved a table for four. An email arrived three weeks later, confirming my table reservation, and giving specific instructions to arrive promptly at 6:15 to sign a release and get instructions. “Be prepared to wait,” the email read, “for the grand reveal. This is TV and anything can happen.”

It wasn't a dream

by FRANZ KAFKA

One morning, when Gregor Samsa woke from troubled dreams, he found himself transformed in his bed into a horrible vermin. He lay on his armour-like back, and if he lifted his head a little he could see his brown belly, slightly domed and divided by arches into stiff sections. The bedding was hardly able to cover it and seemed ready to slide off any moment.

His many legs, pitifully thin compared with the size of the rest of him, waved about helplessly as he looked. "What's happened to me?" he thought. It wasn't a dream. His room, a proper human room although a little too small, lay peacefully between its four familiar walls. A collection of textile samples lay spread out on the table - Samsa was a travelling salesman - and above it there hung a picture that he had recently cut out of an illustrated magazine and housed in a nice, gilded frame. It showed a lady fitted out with a fur hat and fur boa who sat upright, raising a heavy fur muff that covered the whole of her lower arm towards the viewer. Gregor then turned to look out the window at the dull weather.
